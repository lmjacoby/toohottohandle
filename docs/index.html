A Particularly Devastating Year for Wildfires

Spacially and Temporally Understanding Wildfires

Every year in the US, millions of wildfire emerge and cause unimaginable damage to people and places. In this first narrative we are going to explore the size and location of wildfires during 2018, which was a particularly devastating year for wildfires. In the map below, you are able to visualize the fires of 2018 through data taken by a NASA satellite. You can scroll on your own to focus on a particular time of the year, or just watch the animation on autoplay. The points that appear on the map are the force radiative power measure in mW per unit area. The larger and more red spots indicate more powerful wildfires detected at that location.

The Summer of Fire

The months in the summer are typically the most catastrophic for wildfires and 2018 was no different. Ordered by "Month", the distribution is Gaussian-like, with lowest acres burned occuring at the beginning and end of the year (the winter months). Into Spring, the total acres burned starts to pick up and then explodes in summer. July and August alone account for ~50% of the acres burned that year. Then as the fall comes, the burned acres drop back down heading toward another winter. Change the drop down menu to "Order" and see the absolute order of highest and lowest burn months.

The Biggest Fires are in the West

This map of the US shows total burned acres by state in 2018, with darker reds indicating higher values. Hover over a state to learn how many total acres were burned that year. It is clear states in the Western part of the US experienced the most fire damage. Overlayed on the US map is a bubble map which indicates the location of the 50 largest fires (total acres burned) in 2018, with radius of the bubble representing total size of the fire. Not only did the west have the most fire, but it also had the worst fires. To learn the name, size, and dates of the fifty largets fires, hover on the black bubbles.

Most Wildfires are Cause by Humans

Wildfires are overwhelming caused by humans. This radial stacked bar chart explores the causes of wildfires in 2018 broken down by state. The US Forest Service classifies fires into three main categories: human, natural, or undetermined. Some of the causes of human fires are: arson, fireworks and explosives and smoking.

How are US Wildfires Changing Over Time?

Looking at yearly wildfire data from the past three decades, 1992-2018, can help us understand if and how wildfires have changed with time, and what that means for the future.

Wildfires are Getting Worse

Over the past three decades, the number of acres burned every year has statistically been increasing. Between 1992-1996, an average of 3.3 million acres burned per year due to wild fires. Two decades later, between 2014-2018 that average doubles to 7.5 million acres per year. The cause could be related to climate change, which has created a warmer world with drier summers which lead to more fires.

Larger Fires Are Increasing in Time

Not only are more acres burning per year, but larger fires are the culprit. The US Forest Service classifies fires into seven categories. Move the scrubber on the bar chart to see how the relative amount of larger fires (class G) have changed over three decases. Fire classes are:

  • Class A: 0.25 acres or less
  • Class B: > 0.25 acres, < 10 acres
  • Class C: >= 10 acres, < 100 acres
  • Class D: >= 100 acres, < 300 acres
  • Class E: >= 300 acres, < 1,000 acres
  • Class F: >= 1,000 acres, < 5,000 acres
  • Class G: >= 5,000 acres
